You suffer from a mild manic-depressive condition, where the oppressive meaninglessness of the universe often either brings you low or drives you to bursts of desperate energy.
Faction: Bleak Cabal.
Benefit: At the beginning of every day, roll 1d20. On a result of 1 or lower you are depressed and can only perform a single standard action per round for the day, but you gain a +4 circumstance bonus on Will saving throws. On a roll between 2 and 20 you are manic, gaining a +2 morale bonus to any attack rolls and a -2 circumstance penalty on saving throws. You may choose to add or subtract your Wisdom modifier (if positive) from this roll, but a natural roll of 1 always results in depression and a natural roll of 20 always results in a manic state. If you ever suffer through three consecutive days of madness (either a manic state or depression), you must make a Will save (DC 20) or progress further into insanity. For every consecutive day of madness past the first three requires another Will save with a cumulative +4 to the DC. A failed save means you gain an Insanity score (see below).
Special: Upon failing the Will save you gain an Insanity score equal to ½ your character level. For spellcasting (determining bonus spells and DCs), you use your Wisdom score plus your Insanity score in place of Wisdom alone. For all other purposes, such as skills and saves, use Wisdom minus Insanity in place of Wisdom.
Being treated for insanity requires a Bleaker with at least 8 ranks of Heal to treat the character, making a Heal check at the end of each week of treatment (DC 15 + the patient's Will save modifier without Insanity modifiers). Each successful consecutive weeks of treatment allow the patient to lose one from their Insanity rating. A failed week of treatment results in temporary Wisdom damage equal to the patient's Insanity rating. Treatment cannot be performed while the patient has Wisdom damage of any sort.
Once per day, you can see and act with the clarity of true madness. Use your Insanity score as a positive rather than a negative modifier on a single roll involving Wisdom, such as Listen check or a Will saving throw. Choose to use this power before the roll is made.

LOL.

First off, I assure you we're not attempting to delude anyone. There are a couple feats that have clear penalties associated with them, such as the Doomguard feat that forces you to resist healing. The Bleaker feat is a little more vague because of its complexity and the requirement that we explain the Insanity System, but I believe it is compensated by the feats that come from it which are pretty damn good.

Now then, I'm very glad we're getting to the heart of the matter and leaving out attacks (for the most part). I think you may be pleased when we release Variant Rules for using belief mechanically. It won't ever be official, as its been voted down too many times, but the mere presence of mechanical benefits/penalties for belief I hope will get the point across about how important and powerful belief is.

In regards to the factions, I think I can understand how you feel. However, it is my belief that this issue is better addressed through role-playing and advise (in the form of fluffing up the beginning of Chapter 3) rather than mechanics. The problem with doing things mechanically is that 1) the mechanics we create may not conform with how people wish to view their character/faction and 2) the penalties are impossible to balance. With the feats we could give people options on how to take their factionhood, but penalties aren't as flexible.

There is indeed something to your statement about awarding mechanical bonuses for dealing with those within your faction. One might be inclined to stat out penalties for enemies, but we're really not ready to detail the inter-faction rivalries in such detail at this point. Then there's the fact that having a mechanical system doesn't take into account rivals within the same faction. I think that line might be better off removed and left as a role-playing feature.

As for the feats, Chapter 4 states that those who lose faith in the beliefs of their faction will lose access to the feats (if they are Faction-Dependent), however it may be prudent to go indepth to some degree as to what might constitute losing faith. A lot of this stuff I've intended to leave for the DM's section as a guide to how the planes should be run, but it's probably good to help the players understand as well.

The heart of the matter is there's a lot of philosophy and thought that needs to be put into a role-playing character that can be pushed, but not enforced. It's like Mage the Ascension; the game presents a universal system that defies any "right" way of doing magic, and then they have a completely role-playing based paradigm element that requires the players to view their magic as more than a Life 5 effect and actually restrict it to what their characters believe magic can do. It's one of the most difficult aspects of the game to get, largely because it doesn't interfer with the universal magic system and because its so completely free form. To enforce it arbitrarily restricts the system, but being lax makes it harder to understand.

Chapter 2 will eventually start off character creation guiding players through creating a character based off what they believe and how they live their life. Chapter 3 will include more about what it means to be in a faction, the good and the bad. Chapter 4 will expound upon how faction abilities manifest in relation to one's belief.

Other than that, I don't know what we can do.

More validation for my stance...

"Behind the Curtain: Test-Based Prerequisites

So why don't all prestige classes employ test-based prerequisites? It's a matter of control that highlites the fundamental difference between the job of the game designer and the job of the DM. The D&D rules ue the prerequisites they do because they're based on predictable numbers. No character can have a base attack bonus higher than her character level, for example, or more ranks in a skill than her character level +3. For the game designer, that predictability is an important balancing tool, ensuring that no character gets access to a prestige class or feat so early that it makes the game less fun.

But as the DM, you don't have to have to worry about millions of possible characters, just the specific ones in your campaign. You can predict your players better than anyone else, both in terms of what appeals to them and what means they use to achieve those ends. And you are the architect of the game world. If a PC becomes and eldritch knight by "cheating" on the entrance test, the knights might kick him out and update their test to close the loophole - just as a real-world organization would."

Unearthed Arcana, page 212.

Emboldened and italicized text is my emphasis.

Insideous Magic
You can use the Shadow Weave to make your spells harder for weave users to detect. All creatures employing spells or spell-like abilities are considered to be Weave-Users unless they possess the Shadow Magic Feat
Prerequisite: Shadow Weave Magic
Benefit: When a weave user emplays a divination spell, spell-like ability, or magic item that may detect the magical aura of one of your spells, the Weave user must make a level check (DC 11 + Your caster level) to successfully detect your spells… This benefit does not extend to spells you cast from the schools of Evocation or Transmution…
