There is no prime/outer split in BCD. All places are separate planes not necessarily connected to each other in any geometric or even metaphysical way. Some planes may be in conjunction with other planes for a while, then move away. There is no map of the planes as there is for the Great Wheel and the Inner Planes; each plane simply exists in an infinity of countless worlds (hence the name of the book).

Frankly I would have preferred to see a map of the conjoined planes, but that's the geographer in me. It's more mysterious this way.

BCD does have several Great Paths though, one being the Ethereal Sea (which is not quite like the standard Ethereal), the other being the, uhh, Underworld (IIRC). DMs can make up other ones too, like the Ports that James mentioned. I would like to see us meet in a multi-dimensional tavern akin to the World Serpent Inn or the Bazaar from the Myth series.

What we'll be doing is plane-hopping from one dimension to the next, maybe picking up people as we go along.

Just don't go to any dimension marked as the Ten Courts of Hell. Eek.

Without writing myself into a constrained position: there will almost always be another plane to shunt through for spell/power purposes. Certainly for the first couple planes that we've planned to start the campaign I can definitively say there are planes to dimension door through.
Krypter made a good description of the travel planes in BCD: "Regardless, these planes are not universally transitive; they can only connect planes that are in conjunction with them." There are planes that one would probably consider transitive like the Ethereal Sea, the Underground, and the Nexus, that is planes where there are few landmarks and connected to lots of planes. Planes like that seem built for transit, but that's where the distinction comes in. In Planescape there are three planes (the Astral, the Ethereal, and arguably the Shadow) which are designed for transit in a cosmogenic sense. They're purpose is providing connections because that is what they formed to do. In the Countless Worlds there are planes which, though randomly formed like all other planes, lend themselves remarkably to travel in the way that the Mediterranean fortuitously provides easy transport on Earth. The Ethereal Sea didn't form as a metaphysical highway, it came to be a highway because of its utility.
I think the best way to think of "transitive planes" in this campaign is to forget completely about the Astral, etc., and think of equivalents to the Rivers Oceanus and Styx. These are handy pathways that canny travelers use, but they only go to a few places.
The standard explanation for Shadowjump and similar abilities is to say that the user slips into the cracks between the Countless Worlds, cracks where nothing exists, and can find shortcuts this way.

I did notice that and my opinion is that it's a step away from the philosophy of Planescape. BCD is reality-based rather than belief-based (you can't, for instance, shift part of Deleur into Avidarel by creating a lot of nightmare-shadows). I think deities either exist meta-planarly and have widespread but weakened influence or else they limit themselves to one plane, such as Mother Ocean or the Sleeping Goddess, and have greater influence. Or, if you like, the more direct power they wish to command (or find themselves with) the smaller the area they can reasonably concentrate on.
The attitude in BCD seems similar to the attitude of Eberron, that deities have limited direct influence on the world to put a greater emphasis on mortals. It's difficult, for instance, for the Silver Flame to have a crusade (Eberron) or for the formians to conquer the entirity of Dendri (BCD) if there are Powers involved, it just turns into a godwar. This is not to condemn Planescape theology, of course, it's just a way to have different games.

My character is not yet complete. The base mechanics are all done, but I haven't picked feats, skills, or bought equipment (this is the stuff that always slows me down).

http://3eprofiler.net/3ep/view.php?id=18635

Don Lithec was born to the Steel Eaters tribe of dire kobolds, a culturally isolated group dwelling on the coasts of the Great Eastern Plains. The plains kobolds developed a highly complex and rigid society based upon honor and martial prowess. Their cities were built around great monasteries/hatcheries/Temples to the Egg Mother and a stratified caste system similar to many of the "Oriental-style" cultures of some mammal planes.

Don Lithec was the child of a noble warrior family and, as such, was expected to follow in the footsteps of his ancestors. He trained rigorously in the arts of combat (which he excelled) and in the more contemplative and noble arts expected of a warrior of his stature (in which he did not excel). Oh, he was a bright student and his natural charisma made him an excellent taleteller, but underneath all of that his understanding of the central tenants of the bushi was tenuous and his tea ceremony was always atrocious.

In any case his battle prowess and quickness of body more than made up for his metaphysical deficits. He was feared amongst the expansionist formians of the area and had a number of large kills to is name, not the least of which was a Tyrannosaur that had destroyed the egg chamber of a nearby monastery.

He came to the planes by accident. An adventuring expedition into the great sea carried his ship into a swirling maelstrom. He was tossed overboard by the crashing waves and dragged down into the depths. When he awoke he found himself on dry land surrounded by a herd of unusually large nightfolk. The nightfolk took him in and nursed him back to health. He had washed up on the shores of a great planar metropolis... somehow - a world that had never heard of his realm. Now, he hunts amongst the planes populated by massive vermin searching for his home again. He is no rush, however, his plans are currently to explore as much as possible before returning, to bring knowledge of the worlds outside to his people.
