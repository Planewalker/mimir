I think a lot of sci-fi tropes, especially utopia/dystopia considerations/permutations would actually be enacted in Planescape.

=-=-=

"VR originated within marginal subcultures: from science fiction, cyberpunk, and computer hacker culture, and from institutions including NASA, computer companies, and the military. Perceiving much wider applications than flight simulation and remote control, researchers coined the term “virtual reality,” and promoted it as a paradigm shift for computers, and even for the whole society. The shift, though, was not into empty terrain: it was into such existing fields as entertainment, art, architecture, design and medicine.… Moving from marginal cultural tributaries into the cultural mainstream, though, VR itself had to change; it needed to remove its uncomfortable associations with social criticism, drugs and insanity"
-Chris Chesser

I think a lot of our modern entertainment concepts, including/especially television, would be reproduced in Sigil via magic akin to recorder stones. Of course "Reality TV" would range from inter-exemplar dating to harrowing dives into the Abyss or Inner Planes.

Being an accomplished illusionist is less useful for conflicts in a Multiverse where lots of beings are immune to your effects. Seems to me that would lead to a growth in the "VR" industry as well.

Of course, the popularity of these things might vary as well. If you have a chance to be part of an observer determined reality the entertainment provided by the Scifi/Fantasy genre may not hold as much appeal to you.

At the same time, I could see soap operas and the like commanding the peoples' attention, and groups like the Incantifiers would probably want to talk about the "Future Benefits of Magic" in the way Cosmos is currently trying to get people interested in the benefits of scientific exploration.

Plato’s Realm of Forms:

"Plato’s theory of forms (or ideas) lies at the heart of his philosophy. It follows on directly from his allegory of the cave and understanding reality. One of the problems that philosophers tried to answer in ancient Greece was that of relating the many to the one. For example when we see a particular chair for the first time how do we know it is a chair? The particular example of the chair we are presented with might not bear any resemblance to any other chair we have seen before yet we know instinctively that it is a chair..."

The stars that caught our blind amphibian stare have shifted far or vanished in their courses, but still that naked, glistening thread winds onward.
